## What hop count actually measures

When a packet leaves a source node in MeshCore, it does not always travel directly to its destination. Repeaters — dedicated forwarding nodes — carry the packet toward the observer, with each repeater appending its node hash to the packet's path record before retransmitting. By the time the packet reaches your enrolled observer, the path field records exactly which repeaters it passed through and in what order. Hop count is the length of that record.

One hop means the source was in direct radio range of the observer. Two hops means one repeater was involved. Three hops means two repeaters. The count is not estimated; it is a count of the actual relay legs the packet used.

Because MeshCore clients do not repeat — forwarding is handled by dedicated repeater nodes — this path record is reliable. A hop count of three means three real radio links, three real nodes that had to receive and retransmit, three independent failure points. The count tells you exactly how much infrastructure each packet depends on.

## Where Waev surfaces hop count

Three surfaces, each for a different question.

**Live Packets** shows the hop count on every individual observation as it arrives. Each row includes the source node, timestamp, hop count, and SNR. If you want to know how many hops a specific node is currently taking — right now, this minute — Live Packets is where you look.

**Network Stats** aggregates into a path-quality view. Instead of individual packet rows, you see one row per active node with its typical hop count, recent SNR, and a status flag: healthy, watchlist, or degraded. Network Stats is the faster way to triage your entire network. One scan shows which nodes are operating near their baseline and which have drifted into elevated counts or poor SNR.

**Outpost** is the per-node deep-dive: hop count and SNR history over days and weeks. This is where the creep pattern becomes visible. A snapshot tells you where hop count is now; Outpost's trend view tells you where it has been — and that difference is the diagnostic.

## What the numbers mean

Hop count guidance depends on your specific topology, but some reference points apply broadly:

**1–2 hops.** The comfortable baseline. The packet reaches your observer with minimal relay involvement. Few links means fewer places to fail.

**3–4 hops.** Normal for geographically spread networks with multiple infrastructure relays. Three or four hops is not a problem on its own. A node that has always been at this count is not a candidate for action. A node that recently moved there from two hops is.

**5+ hops.** Workable but worth attention. Latency accumulates at each forwarding step, and the probability that at least one hop degrades under adverse conditions rises. If five-plus hops has been stable since initial deployment, the question is whether better relay placement could improve the path. If the count is new, something changed.

The number that matters most is change, not the absolute value. A node consistently at three hops is fine. A node that moved from two hops to four hops is routing around something, and that something is worth finding.

## Hop count creep: reading the trend

Hop count creep is when a node's count increases over time without a deliberate change to the network. It is one of the most reliable early warnings a mesh gives you — not because it announces failure, but because it shows a path becoming less reliable before it actually fails.

The mechanism is straightforward. As a relay's performance degrades — an antenna that shifted, a power supply showing intermittent drops, an RF environment change — the network starts preferring alternative paths. Those alternatives typically route through more hops. The node does not disappear from your observer; it keeps arriving, but over longer routes. The hop count record in Outpost makes this visible days or weeks before the path fails entirely.

The pattern in the chart above is the one to learn: a long stable baseline, followed by a flicker that recovers, followed by a gradual step up that stabilizes at a new level. The first flicker is noise or a transient condition. The sustained elevation is the signal — the network encoding, in hop count data, that a shorter path is no longer reliable.

## Turning the measurement into action

Hop count data tells you that something changed in the routing; it does not automatically tell you what. The next step is finding the relay that was in the shorter path and is no longer being heard reliably.

**When hop count creeps from 2 to 3, or 3 to 4 over days:** open Live Packets history for that node and look at what relays appeared in the earlier, shorter-hop observations. That is the candidate relay — its power supply, antenna, and recent packet activity are where you look next.

**When hop count jumps suddenly in a single day:** the previous path failed abruptly rather than degraded gradually. The relay in the short path stopped being reachable. Check that relay's uptime and connection state first.

**When multiple nodes simultaneously show increased hop count:** a shared relay is almost certainly the cause. Identify which relay was in the normal path for several of the affected nodes — the one that appears most often in earlier Live Packets records for the affected group — and investigate that relay first. A single well-placed relay, restored or replaced, will bring the hop counts back down for all of them.

Both signals together — hop count and SNR — give you the complete path quality picture. If hop count is elevated and SNR on the new longer path is marginal, you have a node at the edge of coverage whose current routing is fragile. That combination is the one worth acting on before it fails at an inconvenient time.
