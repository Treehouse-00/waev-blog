If you're running a mesh network for a community that depends on it — CERT team, ham club, off-grid neighborhood, emergency response group — you already know this is real infrastructure. Quiet, unglamorous, and genuinely important.

<span class="waev-brand">Waev</span> exists because people like you needed a way to understand what those networks are actually doing: live packets, signal per hop, coverage, the health of the infrastructure you keep running, often alone, often without much tooling.

We thought carefully about what this tool should be, and wrote it down — because you deserve to know where we stand.

> A tool shaped by strategy is more useful than one shaped by whoever asked last.

Here are the six values underneath everything else.

## 01 · Privacy is the default, not a dial

The conversations on your mesh are real. The people having them trust the infrastructure you built. We don't take that lightly.

Two rules do most of the work. First, **opt-out is absolute**: add a privacy marker — ⛔ 🛑 🚫 — to any node name and it is never stored, never mapped, never counted. No caching, no edge cases, no exceptions. Second, **companion devices are scrubbed before anything is written**. MeshCore personal apps broadcast on the same radio mesh as your repeaters, so those packets reach our observers. At the ingest edge, we strip the identity — name and device type are nullified — and retain only anonymous signal metadata for aggregate counting.

<Figure caption="<b>Left:</b> a node that opts out is dropped entirely. <b>Right:</b> a companion ADVERT keeps its signal numbers, loses its identity.">

</Figure>

If you're running a companion app, you won't appear on <span class="waev-brand">Waev</span>. The only personal devices we ever surface are ones an operator explicitly chose to connect — and even then, a privacy marker in the name overrides everything.

Privacy here isn't a setting. It's the floor.

## 02 · Evidence over inference

When you look at your network map, everything on it should be there because something verifiable put it there. Not because we guessed.

Every edge in the topology traces back to something we can confirm: an **enrolled observer** connected over MQTT, or an **authenticated repeater**. A spoofed or inferred prefix doesn't get a line on the map — it gets rejected.

<Figure caption="Verified sources draw an edge. Inference doesn't — <b>no proof, no line.</b>">

</Figure>

That makes the map less crowded, sometimes. But when it shows you something, you can trust it. That's worth more to us than a busier screen.

## 03 · Aesthetics are a feature

You've probably used dashboards that made the network seem more confusing than it actually is. Charts that don't explain themselves. Colors that mean nothing. Density for density's sake. We have too.

A dashboard that's hard to read is *wrong*, not just ugly. Clarity is part of correctness. Color has to mean something. Motion has to explain, not decorate. If a screen can't make a complex thing feel simple, it isn't finished.

This is a constraint we work under from the first sketch — not polish bolted on at the end. The practical payoff: when something is wrong on your network, you should see it immediately. Not after squinting.

## 04 · Performance is the point

"Real-time" is a promise. Packets should appear the instant they arrive. A map should pan without lag. A query that covers your whole mesh should come back before you wonder if it's stuck.

There's a real difference between a tool you have to check and a tool you can actually watch. We're building the second kind, and we hold that bar even when it's expensive to do so.

## 05 · Your community's data stays yours

You built your mesh. You recruited the operators, configured the nodes, solved the RF problems, and kept it running. That community — and its data — belongs to you.

The old model, where everyone requests access to a central set of MQTT servers, turns into a management problem fast: scoped here, full access there, a half-dozen requests a day, every one a judgment call. We've watched it happen. We'd rather invert it entirely.

**You bring your users to a broker you own. We subscribe, and keep a read-only copy.**

<Figure caption="You host the broker and own the data. <b>We take a copy, not the keys.</b>">
  <BrokerCopy />
</Figure>

You keep full control. We get what we need to build useful tools. Nobody manages an access list. It's a better arrangement for everyone — and it happens to be the honest one.

## 06 · Opinionated, on purpose

<span class="waev-brand">Waev</span> is built deliberately, in an order that serves the network as a whole — not one shaped by whoever asked most recently. We want to be honest about that upfront.

If you send us a bug report, we take it seriously. Always. If you send a feature suggestion, we'll read it carefully, and we might build it, and we might not, and we'll be straight with you about which. Saying no thoughtfully is a form of respect — it means we're not trading the long-term quality of the tool for short-term goodwill.

A considered roadmap is a feature too.

## Thank you

Mesh networks don't maintain themselves. The people who run them — who tune the RF, place the repeaters, recruit the members, keep it alive through outages and upgrades and all the invisible work in between — are doing something that matters. We see it.

<span class="waev-brand">Waev</span> exists to be worthy of that. These six values are how we try to stay honest with ourselves about what that means.

Found something broken? Tell us. Bug reports are the best feedback we get, and we genuinely mean that.
